UAE AI Governance — The Tahnoon Concentration
The UAE's AI portfolio — G42, MGX, ADIA, IHC, the Abu Dhabi Integrated Technology and Communications Council (AIATC), and First Abu Dhabi Bank, combined above $1.3 trillion — sits under one person within a broader family governance structure, where Sheikh Tahnoon bin Zayed chairs all of them at the same time. The wider structure, known as the Bani Fatima collective (the brothers of UAE President Mohammed bin Zayed who hold major state and commercial roles), spreads authority across family members so that no single removal or succession event would bring down the entire system.

Within that collective, however, the AI portfolio has no institutional depth — roughly twelve individuals hold overlapping board seats across all these entities, and a January 2026 reorganisation moved non-AI assets elsewhere while leaving every AI-related holding under Tahnoon. Three entities in this network have recorded personnel and corporate overlaps with DarkMatter, a US-sanctioned surveillance firm, including G42's current CEO Peng Xiao — a Chinese business executive whose prior roles and associations have been examined by the US House of Representatives and the Associated Press. Saudi Arabia — Singular Control, No Separation of Governance Functions
Saudi Arabia's AI governance runs through one individual with no separation between its regulatory, investment, and commercial functions — Mohammed bin Salman personally chairs SDAIA (the national AI regulator), PIF (the $913 billion investment fund), HUMAIN (the national AI company), and NEOM. Unlike the UAE's distribution of portfolios across family members, this structure has no identified backup — a single succession event or extended absence would disrupt every major AI entity in the chain. PIF's own governor described the fund on national television as operating "outside the outlines of governance."

Human Rights Watch identified specific instances in which MBS overrode unanimous board opposition to force through major financial decisions. NEOM offers a reference point for how Saudi mega-programmes have tracked against their original plans: its scope shrank from 170 kilometres to 2.4 kilometres, its estimated cost grew from $500 billion to $8.8 trillion, and construction was suspended in September 2025.

Qatar — Opacity, and a National AI Entity That Is Currently a Name
Qatar's governance model sits between the UAE's family collective and Saudi Arabia's single-person authority — the Emir governs AI by delegation through the Prime Minister's office, producing a more spread-out structure than either neighbour but one where the public record is thin. Qai, Qatar's national AI entity, was launched in December 2025 chaired by Abdullah bin Hamad Al Misnad — but as of February 2026 it still has no appointed CEO, no disclosed executive team, no published governance structure, and a one-page website, while its $20 billion joint venture with Brookfield has no disclosed location, capacity target, ownership split, or timeline.

Qai has explicitly stated it will not build its own large language models, instead evaluating and commercialising existing ones — a strategic distinction from the UAE's G42/Falcon and Saudi Arabia's HUMAIN/ALLaM approaches that positions Qatar as a capital allocator rather than a model developer. Qatar has committed $14.5 billion in technology investments since early 2024, including a five-year partnership with Scale AI covering 50 government use cases and an AI Sandbox with OpenAI and PwC. The TASMU Smart Qatar Program, with a budget of approximately $1.65 billion, has deployed AI across five priority sectors with 114 digital use cases running on Microsoft Azure — representing Qatar's most tangible operational AI deployment, though it is cloud-based rather than sovereign compute.

This assessment applies the same evidence standard to Qatar that it applies to Saudi Arabia and the UAE: entities that have been announced but show no operational activity are not treated as functioning institutions. GOVERNANCE OPACITY — ADDITIONAL FINDINGS
QIA scores 46 out of 100 on the Sovereign Wealth Fund Transparency Scoreboard — despite having co-authored the Santiago Principles that the scoreboard measures against. The fund's chairman, Sheikh Bandar bin Mohammed, simultaneously serves as Governor of Qatar's Central Bank, combining sovereign investment decision authority with financial regulatory oversight in a single role.

The Shura Council, which provided a nominal accountability mechanism within Qatar's governance structure, was discontinued in November 2024. Taken together, the transparency gap, the dual-role concentration, and the removal of the accountability body form the basis for treating Qatar's governance opacity as an established pattern rather than a simple absence of information.

Bilateral Commitment Maturity — Frameworks Are Not Flows
Gulf AI deals are announced in round billions, but whether those numbers represent money that has actually moved, contracts that are legally binding, or pledges that may or may not convert is a question the headline figures do not answer on their own. This assessment groups commitments into four levels: Operational (capital deployed with independently verifiable infrastructure), Contracted (binding legal agreements with named counterparties and defined deliverables), Framework (a head-of-state pledge with a dollar figure but no contracted projects), and Aspirational (conference announcements and memoranda of understanding, of which roughly 30–50 per cent have historically converted to binding deals).

France and Canada both have figures in the vicinity of $50 billion associated with UAE AI — France's commitment sits at the Contracted level (named counterparties, binding agreements, 1.4 gigawatts of committed capacity), while Canada's sits at the Framework level (a head-of-state pledge with no contracted projects identified below it).

Diagram 1.5 · Section 1.3 Technology Architecture
Technology Dependency Chain — Approved ≠ Delivered ≠ Installed ≠ Operational
Every Gulf AI programme runs on US-manufactured chips, US-operated cloud platforms, and US export control rules that determine who receives chips, in what quantities, and under what conditions — but the specific rules governing each country's chip use are not yet publicly available for any of the three states. Beneath the American computing layer sits a Chinese telecom layer: Huawei built the 5G networks that all three countries use, including the networks data travels through to reach AI data centres, and no outside technical assessment of how well these two layers are kept separate has been published.

The US approved 116,900 chips for the UAE, but G42's chairman confirmed in December 2025 the country physically had "a couple of thousand" — Saudi Arabia and Qatar have approved amounts on paper with no comparable public statement about actual delivery. Any Canadian co-investment in this technology chain inherits every dependency in the layers below it.

PAX SILICA — The US-Led Multilateral AI Architecture (Dec 2025)
Launched December 12, 2025 by the US State Department under Under Secretary Jacob Helberg, Pax Silica is the flagship multilateral initiative for AI and semiconductor supply chain security. Its core mechanism: member states gain preferred access to advanced AI chips within a framework designed to maintain a permanent US-China technology lead — what its architects describe as the "eighteen-month moving gap" doctrine.
The founding signatories were Japan, South Korea, Singapore, the Netherlands, the United Kingdom, Israel, and Australia — seven countries spanning Asia, Europe, and Oceania. Qatar acceded on January 12, 2026, and the UAE followed three days later on January 15, bringing the total membership to nine states. India was formally invited and is expected to sign in February 2026. Canada attended the launch as a guest and observer and has not signed as of February 2026.

The European Union also chose not to sign the agreement at the time of its launch. CEPA (Centre for European Policy Analysis) assessed the resulting membership pattern as indicating that the initiative prioritises "political trust and perceived strategic alignment" over technological capacity alone. The framework is non-binding but aims to create trusted supply chains and counter China's approximately 90 per cent control of rare earth processing.

WHAT IS THE CLEAN NETWORK — Definition
The Clean Network was a US State Department initiative launched in 2020 that required participating countries to exclude Chinese technology companies — primarily Huawei and ZTE — from their telecommunications infrastructure, specifically fifth-generation networks. The programme established a set of trusted vendors and required signatories to route all carrier networks through non-Chinese equipment. The UAE was the only Gulf state to join the Clean Network initiative; Saudi Arabia and Qatar chose not to participate.

Clean Network membership is relevant to this assessment because it determines whether the telecommunications infrastructure carrying data to and from AI data centres relies on Chinese-built equipment. All three Gulf states continue to operate Huawei-built fifth-generation networks regardless of their formal membership status, and no independent technical assessment has verified that the UAE's membership has changed the underlying Huawei network architecture. The distinction is therefore one of formal commitment rather than operational reality — the UAE holds a pledge to exclude Chinese vendors that Saudi Arabia and Qatar do not.

Five Eyes Comparison — Where Canada Stands Relative to Its Partners
Canada's position in the Gulf AI environment is defined by three structural absences: no contracted capital, no presence in either major allied AI security architecture (Pax Silica (the US-led multilateral AI and semiconductor supply chain framework — see Where Canada Stands tab) or AUKUS Pillar II), and no bilateral investment treaty with any of the three Gulf states under assessment. Canada's Five Eyes partners provide the natural comparison frame for this assessment, and the structural differences between Canada and its closest allies in the Gulf AI domain are substantial.
Australia
Strictest Allied Investment Screening
Australia's Foreign Investment Review Board applies a nil monetary threshold for foreign government investors — any investment, regardless of size, triggers mandatory review. That threshold represents the most stringent allied investment screening mechanism identified in this assessment. Beyond investment screening, Australia holds founding membership in both Pax Silica and AUKUS Pillar II, placing it inside both major allied AI security architectures while maintaining the tightest inbound investment screen among the Five Eyes partners.
United States
Supplier, Regulator, and Investor in a Single Role
The United States occupies every layer of the Gulf AI stack at once: it sets chip export rules through the IGAA, operates as the largest commercial partner in all three Gulf AI ecosystems, and convenes the multilateral compliance architecture through Pax Silica. Congressional scrutiny and active commercial embrace proceed in parallel — the House Select Committee's investigation into G42 governance runs alongside NVIDIA partnership expansions that continue to deepen across all three Gulf states. That coexistence is not a contradiction — it reflects the structural reality of a country that serves concurrently as supplier, regulator, and investor, and whose commercial interests do not always align with its strategic ones.

The UK maintains deeper defence relationships with Gulf states than Canada and possesses institutional mechanisms absent from the Canadian toolkit: AUKUS Pillar II for AI, quantum, and cyber cooperation (from which Canada is excluded), and QUAIRIA — a bilateral AI research commission with Qatar launched during the Emir's December 2024 State Visit. No equivalent bilateral AI cooperation mechanism between Canada and any Gulf state has been found in the public record, and no UK-UAE bilateral AI cooperation agreement has surfaced either — a finding about the bilateral landscape rather than any limitation in UK capability.

Limited Direct Gulf AI Engagement Identified
No direct New Zealand Gulf AI bilateral framework has been found in open sources. New Zealand participates in Five Eyes intelligence sharing and AUKUS adjacent arrangements but is not a full AUKUS Pillar II member. Its Gulf AI engagement, if any, operates below the threshold of public documentation in sources available to this assessment.

Gulf Defense AI — An Emerging Dimension
The UAE's EDGE Group has emerged as the preeminent Gulf defence AI actor, ranked 22nd globally among arms companies by SIPRI with 216 unique products across 35 entities. The JENIAH stealth unmanned combat aerial vehicle — first test-flown in March 2024 at 1,050 kilometres per hour — uses AI for target identification, navigation, and mission execution, and can operate in autonomous group formations. The EDGE-Anduril Production Alliance (November 2025) established a joint venture in the UAE for autonomous systems, with Anduril's Lattice AI software integrated into 50 Omen autonomous air vehicles ordered by UAE forces.

Saudi Arabia is several years behind but investing heavily through GAMI, which targets localising 50 per cent or more of military spending by 2030. At the February 2026 World Defense Show, a senior Saudi official articulated the strategic inflection as whether to innovate with AI-enhanced defence systems or pursue AI-native ones. The US designated the UAE as a "Major Defense Partner" in September 2024 — only the second country after India to receive this designation — and a comprehensive defence partnership was formalised in May 2025.

A critical finding from the Defense AI Observatory: all six GCC national AI strategies remain rather silent on the role of AI for national security and defence. Bahrain is the only Gulf state supporting the US Political Declaration on Responsible Military AI and Autonomy. This silence is itself an intelligence gap — the governance architecture mapped in this assessment's first three panels covers commercial AI, but the defence AI dimension operates through separate channels that are even less transparent.
The India-Gulf AI Corridor — Implications for Five Eyes
The India-Gulf technology relationship has evolved from a labour-supply pipeline to a bilateral innovation corridor, and its scale now has direct implications for Five Eyes intelligence coordination. Approximately 8.8 million Indian nationals reside in GCC countries, comprising over 66 per cent of all Non-Resident Indians globally. The G42-C-DAC 8-exaflop supercomputer represents 19 times India's existing combined peak compute capacity, and the NANDA 87-billion-parameter Hindi-English model targets 600 million speakers. The India-Middle East-Europe Economic Corridor (IMEC), launched at the 2023 G20 Summit, includes a digital pillar anchored by the Blue Raman submarine cable connecting European and Indian academic institutions.

India was formally invited to join Pax Silica and is expected to sign in February 2026, which would place it inside the allied AI architecture that Canada has not joined. India was designated by the US as a "Major Defense Partner" — the same status subsequently extended to the UAE — and the I2U2 framework (India-Israel-UAE-US) provides a US-aligned structure for technology cooperation. The degree to which the India-Gulf AI corridor creates alternative pathways for technology access that could reduce Canada's relative importance as a Five Eyes AI research partner has not been assessed in open-source Canadian analysis.
